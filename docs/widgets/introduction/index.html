Video Understanding

Video Comprehension Score (VCS)

A comprehensive evaluation framework for dense, long-form video descriptions that handles the inherent complexities of narrative text assessment.

The Era of Advanced Video Understanding

Recent advances in Large Video Language Models (LVLMs) have significantly improved automated video understanding, enabling models to interpret increasingly lengthy and complex video content. However, a fundamental question emerges: do these models truly understand the video content they describe?

Current evaluation predominantly relies on QA-based benchmarks that assess isolated aspects of comprehension. However, QA-based tasks inherently fall short in assessing holistic comprehension. Accurately answering isolated questions about specific events does not necessarily indicate genuine understanding of the overall narrative or the intricate interplay of events throughout the video.

True Video Comprehension: The Story Capture Challenge

True video comprehension can only be evaluated by asking LVLMs to describe what is happening in the video, then comparing their narratives with human-written captions to assess whether the model was able to capture the "story". If they did, we can confidently say they understood the video.

This brings us to the core problem: how do we score these narrative descriptions? There are multitudes of ways a valid story can be expressed, and current evaluation approaches fall short:

N-gram Metrics

BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR rely on lexical overlap. BLEU measures n-gram precision with brevity penalties, ROUGE emphasizes recall via n-gram overlap and LCS, METEOR integrates synonyms with fragmentation penalties.

Core Problems:

• Rigid lexical matching fails with paraphrasing
• Cannot distinguish valid vs. invalid variations
• Blind to chronological ordering

Embedding-based

BERTScore, MoverScore, SBERT capture semantic similarity beyond lexical matches but struggle with limited context windows. Modern models like nv-embed-v2 offer larger contexts but overlook local alignment.

Core Problems:

• Global embeddings miss local content accuracy
• Context window limitations for long texts
• Chronological coherence ignored

Multimodal Embedding

EMScore and PAC-S use vision-language models like CLIP for visual-text alignment. Effective for short captions but face computational and methodological challenges with dense, extended narratives.

Core Problems:

• Computational scalability issues
• Complex chronology handling failures
• Segment-level coherence struggles

LLM-based

Component-based (AutoDQ, VAD-Score) use semantic extraction; holistic methods (ChatGPT-based) provide overall assessments. Both suffer from calibration issues, prompt sensitivity, and consistency problems.

Core Problems:

• Score calibration ambiguity
• Prompt sensitivity and inconsistency
• Limited interpretability and reproducibility

The Fundamental Gap: Effectively scoring dense, extended video descriptions remains unresolved due to four intertwined complexities that existing metrics cannot handle.

Why Long Description Scoring is Hard

Four fundamental challenges that break traditional evaluation approaches

1. Expressive Variability

Need for Valid vs. Invalid Variation Distinction

Descriptions can differ substantially in wording, structure, and style. Metrics must distinguish between valid linguistic variations (paraphrasing, stylistic differences) and invalid variations (superficial lexical overlap without semantic correspondence).

2. Descriptive Variability

Leniency vs. Strictness Paradox + Length Mismatch

Descriptions exhibit inherent differences in information density, focus, and length. This creates a fundamental paradox and a critical length challenge:

The Descriptive Variability Paradox: Metrics must accommodate valid variations in detail level (brevity vs. verbosity) while also strictly penalizing significant content omissions or hallucinations. This forces a choice between leniency toward variability or strict content alignment—simultaneously supporting both is inherently contradictory. Additionally, descriptive variability leads to huge mismatches in description length, requiring metrics to handle dramatically different text lengths while maintaining fair evaluation.

3. Narrative Variability

Ordering Flexibility Paradox

Descriptions can present acceptable variations in the chronological sequencing of events. This creates another fundamental paradox:

The Narrative Variability Paradox: While strict chronological order is crucial in certain contexts, minor logical reorderings can be permissible in others. Metrics must be capable of supporting both strict and flexible chronological evaluations, despite the inherent conflict between these two needs.

Need for Global-Local Balance

Descriptions can vary in their coherence at both global and local scales. This requires finding the right balance:

The Chronological Variability Challenge: Metrics must penalize globally incorrect ordering even if locally coherent, and vice versa—effectively evaluating coherence across narrative scales. This requires balanced assessment of micro and macro-level chronological integrity.

Introducing Video Comprehension Score (VCS)

To address these intertwined challenges, we introduce the Video Comprehension Score (VCS), specifically designed for comprehensive assessment of dense, long-form video descriptions. VCS adopts the philosophy that true video comprehension involves accurately capturing the narrative, emphasizing semantic and structural evaluations over rigid lexical matching.

Global Alignment Score (GAS)

Measures overall thematic alignment and semantic coherence across entire descriptions through global embedding comparison.

Local Alignment Score (LAS)

Evaluates semantic correspondence and content coverage through chunk-level alignment and best matching algorithms.

Narrative Alignment Score (NAS)

Assesses chronological integrity and narrative flow through distance-based and line-based chronological evaluation.
